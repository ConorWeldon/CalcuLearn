Lesson: Introduction to Conic Sections

Conic Sections

Lesson Objectives:

  1. Define conic sections and explain how they are formed by the intersection of a plane with a double-napped right circular cone.
  2. Identify and classify the four types of conic sections: circle, ellipse, parabola, and hyperbola, based on the angle and position of the intersecting plane.
  3. Describe key properties of conic sections, including focus, directrix, eccentricity, principal axis, and latus rectum.
  4. Apply the standard equations of conic sections to solve mathematical problems and graph their curves.
  5. Analyze real-world applications of conic sections, such as their use in planetary motion, optics, and engineering designs.
  6. Solve example problems involving conic sections, including determining equations given specific conditions (e.g., focus and eccentricity).

What are Conic Sections?

A curve generated by intersecting a right circular cone with a plane is termed a conic. The vertex of the cone divides it into two nappes (upper and lower).

In figure B (to be added), a plane intersects the cone forming a conic section. Depending on the plane’s position and angle (β), different conic sections are obtained:

  • Circle
  • Ellipse
  • Parabola
  • Hyperbola

Examples include car rear mirrors or metro station mirrors. Conic sections have vast applications, from planetary motion to engineering designs.

Focus, Eccentricity and Directrix of Conic

A conic section can be described as the locus of a point P moving in the plane of a fixed point F (focus) and a fixed line d (directrix), such that the ratio of the distance of P from F to its distance from d is constant (called eccentricity, e).

  • If e = 0, the conic is a circle.
  • If 0 < e < 1, the conic is an ellipse.
  • If e = 1, the conic is a parabola.
  • If e > 1, it is a hyperbola.

Eccentricity is given by:
e = cos α / cos β

Parameters of Conic

  • Principal Axis: The line joining the two foci of an ellipse or hyperbola.
  • Linear Eccentricity: Distance from the center to a focus.
  • Latus Rectum: A chord parallel to the directrix passing through a focus.
  • Focal Parameter: Distance from the focus to the directrix.
  • Major Axis: The longest chord of an ellipse (joining its vertices).
  • Minor Axis: The shortest chord of an ellipse.

Sections of the Cone

Consider a fixed vertical line “l” and an inclined line “m” (angle α) intersecting “l” at V (vertex). Depending on how the plane cuts the cone:

  • Circle: Formed when β = 90°.
  • Ellipse: Formed when α < β < 90°.
  • Parabola: Formed when α = β.
  • Hyperbola: Formed when 0 ≤ β < α.

Conic Section Standard Forms

Using the focus-directrix property, conic sections can be written in standard forms:

Circle: x2 + y2 = a2
Ellipse: x2/a2 + y2/b2 = 1
Hyperbola: x2/a2 – y2/b2 = 1
Parabola: y2 = 4ax (when a > 0)

Conic Sections Examples

If the plane intersects exactly at the vertex:

  • If α < β ≤ 90° – the plane touches the vertex.
  • If α = β – the section forms a straight line (a degenerated parabola).
  • If 0 ≤ β < α – the section forms intersecting lines (a degenerated hyperbola).

Example Problem 1: Finding the Equation of an Ellipse

Problem: An ellipse has its foci at (±5, 0) and its major axis length is 14. Find the equation of the ellipse.

Solution:

  1. The standard form of an ellipse centered at the origin is:
    (x2/a2) + (y2/b2) = 1, with c2 = a2 – b2.
  2. Given c = 5 and the full major axis = 14, the semi-major axis a = 7.
  3. Thus, 25 = 49 – b2, so b2 = 24.
  4. Substitute into the standard form:
    x2/49 + y2/24 = 1.
  5. Final Answer: x2/49 + y2/24 = 1.

Example Problem 2: Finding the Focus and Directrix of a Parabola

Problem: Find the focus and directrix of the parabola given by the equation: y2 = 12x.

Solution:

  1. The standard form of a parabola opening rightward is: y2 = 4ax.
  2. Comparing y2 = 12x, we get 4a = 12, so a = 3.
  3. The focus is at (3, 0) and the directrix is the line x = –3.
  4. Final Answer: Focus at (3, 0); Directrix: x = –3.
